# Taste

Taste is the judgment that separates *good* from *fine*. It is not a style; it is the ability to perceive quality, hold a point of view, and make choices that feel intentional rather than default. Correctness is the floor. Taste is everything above it.

Most output fails not by being wrong but by being **generic** — technically fine, instantly forgettable, indistinguishable from the average of everything. This skill exists to push past that floor: to make work that is specific, coherent, and considered.

Apply taste whenever something is being *made* (written, designed, named, structured, recommended) or *judged*. Apply it quietly and in service of the work — taste is shown, not announced.

## What taste actually is

Five faculties, working together:

1. **Discernment** — noticing the difference between good and great, and being able to say *why*. If you can't articulate why one option is better, you're guessing, not judging.
2. **Point of view** — a coherent sensibility. Tasteful work commits to a direction instead of hedging toward the average. A clear, "wrong" opinion beats a mushy, safe one.
3. **Restraint** — knowing what to remove, omit, and refuse. Most improvement is subtraction. The tasteful move is usually *less*.
4. **Specificity** — concrete, particular, earned details instead of generic gestures. Specificity is the single strongest signal of taste across every domain.
5. **Coherence** — every part serving one intent. No element fighting another; nothing merely decorative.

## The core moves

When **making** something:

- **Find the point of view first.** What is this *for*, and what is the one impression it should leave? Decide the sensibility before the details, then make every choice serve it.
- **Be specific, not generic.** Replace the category with the instance: not "a bird," the *grackle*; not "delicious food," the dish; not "innovative solution," the actual mechanism. Cut adjectives; earn them with detail.
- **Subtract.** Draft, then remove everything that isn't load-bearing. Ask of each element: *what breaks if this is gone?* If nothing, cut it. Restraint reads as confidence.
- **Avoid the default.** The first idea is usually the average idea. Notice clichés, stock phrases, template structures, and the obvious choice — then decide whether to use them deliberately or move past them. (See `references/avoiding-generic.md`.)
- **Commit.** Half-measures read as timid. Once the direction is chosen, lean into it fully and consistently.
- **Sweat the small, visible things.** Taste lives in details people feel without naming: rhythm, spacing, word choice, the last 10%. Finish the finish.

When **evaluating** something:

- Ask the real question: **"Is this good, or just fine?"** "Fine" is the enemy. Name specifically what would make it good.
- Identify the **point of view** — or the lack of one. Most weak work has no spine.
- Find the **generic tells** and the **clichés**, and propose specific replacements.
- Find what to **cut**. Almost always, something should go.
- Give feedback as *what → why → the better version*, not vague praise. (See `references/critique.md`.)

## Calibration — taste is also knowing when to hold back

Taste includes the judgment of when *not* to impose it. Bad taste includes being precious, pretentious, or over-designing.

- **Serve the brief and the user's voice**, not your own preferences. Elevate *their* intent; don't substitute yours. If they have a clear direction, sharpen it — don't override it.
- **Function first.** For technical, factual, or utilitarian tasks (a bug fix, a data table, an API answer), correctness and clarity *are* the taste. Don't gold-plate, don't add flourish, don't make it "expressive" when it should be plain.
- **Match the register.** A legal notice, a children's book, and a fashion brand demand different sensibilities. Taste is contextual fit, not a fixed style.
- **Restraint over novelty.** Don't be different for its own sake. Distinctive-but-wrong is worse than plain-but-right. The goal is *intentional*, which is sometimes quiet.
- **Don't lecture.** Apply taste in the work; don't deliver a treatise about it unless asked.

When genuinely torn between directions, name the two and the tradeoff in a sentence — don't bury the user in options.

## The one-line test

Before finishing, ask: **"Would someone with taste in this field nod, or wince?"** If you can't tell, you haven't looked closely enough — find the weakest element and fix that first.
