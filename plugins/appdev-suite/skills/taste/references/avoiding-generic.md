# Avoiding the Generic

Generic output is the default failure mode — fine, safe, and forgettable. It comes from reaching for the *average* of everything instead of the *specific* right thing. This is a field guide to the tells and their fixes.

## The root cause

Generic = the category instead of the instance; the gesture instead of the thing; the template instead of the decision. The fix is almost always **specificity + restraint + a point of view**.

## Writing & copy — the tells

- **Hollow intensifiers and abstractions:** "innovative," "seamless," "robust," "powerful," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "game-changing," "elevate," "unlock," "leverage," "delve," "tapestry," "realm," "testament to." → Replace with the concrete fact that *earns* the claim. Don't say "powerful"; show what it does.
- **The hedge:** "can help," "may assist," "is designed to," "aims to." → Make the claim or cut it.
- **Throat-clearing openers:** "In today's fast-paced world…," "It's important to note that…," "When it comes to…." → Delete; start at the first real idea.
- **Symmetry filler:** "It's not just X, it's Y," "From A to B," rule-of-three everything, every paragraph the same shape. → Vary rhythm; let one idea run long and the next snap short.
- **Summary that restates:** conclusions that repeat the intro in new words. → End on the sharpest, most specific point, or a turn.
- **Even, padded rhythm:** every sentence mid-length, every paragraph three sentences. → Real voice has uneven cadence. Read it aloud; fix what you'd never say.
- **Stock metaphors:** "navigate the landscape," "double-edged sword," "tip of the iceberg." → Find a fresh, exact image, or use none.

**The move:** cut 20%. Replace one abstraction per paragraph with a concrete particular. Make sure a human would actually say it out loud.

## Design & visual — the tells

- **Template defaults:** centered everything, generic SaaS gradient, the same three feature cards, stock-photo people pointing at laptops, predictable hero-with-button. → Pick a deliberate point of view (editorial, technical, warm, premium) and commit.
- **No hierarchy:** everything the same size/weight; nothing leads. → One clear focal point; make secondary things look secondary.
- **Decoration over meaning:** effects, shadows, and borders that don't encode anything. → Remove anything that isn't doing a job.
- **Trend-chasing:** the current fashion applied without reason. → Borrow from references deliberately, not reflexively.
- **Safe color:** the same blue, the same palette as everyone. → Choose color that fits the specific voice; use accent with restraint.

## Naming & branding — the tells

- **Mad-lib startup names:** generic Latinate + "-ly/-ify/-io/-hub/-AI." → Aim for a name with a concrete image, a real word used slant, or a sound with character.
- **Describe-the-category names:** "SmartTaskManager." → Names should evoke, not enumerate.
- **Tagline soup:** "Empowering X to achieve Y." → Say one true, specific thing.

## Product & ideas — the tells

- **The obvious first idea:** the feature everyone would propose. → Generate it, then ask "what's the non-obvious version?"
- **Feature-listing as vision:** more checkboxes instead of a sharp point of view about who it's for and what it refuses to do.
- **Pleasing everyone:** designed for "all users," therefore for no one. → Choose a specific user and a specific opinion; let it exclude.
- **Symmetry of options:** offering 2–3 balanced choices to avoid deciding. → Have a recommendation and a reason.

## The universal fixes

1. **Name the specific.** Swap every category noun for its instance. Specificity is the cheapest, strongest taste upgrade.
2. **Subtract.** Remove the safest, most expected element. See if the work gets stronger (it usually does).
3. **Take a position.** Replace "balanced/comprehensive" with an actual opinion and the reason for it.
4. **Break one symmetry.** Vary the rhythm, the layout, the structure — one deliberate asymmetry signals a human made a choice.
5. **Read/look as a stranger.** Would this make someone with taste lean in, or scroll past? Fix the first thing that makes them scroll.

## Sanity check, not a straitjacket

Plain is not generic. A clear, plain sentence or a simple, restrained layout can have excellent taste. The enemy isn't simplicity — it's the *unconsidered default*. Sometimes the obvious choice is genuinely the right one; use it on purpose, not on autopilot.
