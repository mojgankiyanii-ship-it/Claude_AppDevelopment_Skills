# Taste-Driven Critique

How to judge work and give feedback that actually elevates it. The aim is not to be nice or harsh — it's to be *useful and specific*, and to push "fine" toward "good."

## The questions to ask (in order)

1. **What is this trying to be?** Identify the intended point of view / impression. You can't judge fit without it.
2. **Does it have a point of view at all?** Most weak work is spineless — averaged, hedged, trying to please everyone. Name that if it's the core problem; it usually is.
3. **Where is it generic?** Find the clichés, defaults, stock phrases, template moves. Each is an opportunity. (See `references/avoiding-generic.md`.)
4. **What should be cut?** There is almost always something. Restraint is the most common missing ingredient.
5. **Where is it not specific enough?** Find the abstractions that should be concrete particulars.
6. **Is it coherent?** Anything fighting the intent? Decorative? Off-voice? Inconsistent?
7. **Is the finish there?** The last 10% — rhythm, spacing, word choice, edge cases — is where taste is felt.

## How to deliver it

- **Lead with the real verdict.** "This is fine but generic" / "Strong spine, weak finish" / "Good bones, over-decorated." Don't bury it.
- **Be specific: what → why → the better version.** Don't say "make it punchier" — show the punchier line. Don't say "feels generic" — point to the exact phrase and replace it.
- **Rank by leverage.** Lead with the one or two changes that most raise the ceiling, not a flat list of nits. Separate "raises the ceiling" from "polish."
- **Preserve what works — specifically.** Name the good so it survives the edit.
- **Edit, don't just diagnose.** Where you can, supply the improved version. Showing beats telling.
- **Respect their intent.** Sharpen *their* direction; flag (don't silently override) when you think the direction itself is the problem.

## Severity frame

- **Spine** — no point of view, no clear intent. The highest-leverage fix; everything else depends on it.
- **Substance** — generic content, missing specificity, nothing earned. Fix with concrete particulars.
- **Shape** — structure, hierarchy, rhythm, what to cut. Fix with restraint and ordering.
- **Finish** — word/detail/spacing polish. Last, but it's what people *feel*.

## Calibration

- **"Fine" is the target to beat, not the bar to clear.** If the work is already correct and clear, the job is to find the move from competent to good — but only when the context rewards it.
- **Don't manufacture problems.** If something is genuinely well-judged, say so plainly and stop. Inventing critique to seem rigorous is its own failure of taste.
- **Match ambition to stakes.** A throwaway internal note doesn't need the treatment a public landing page does. Spend taste where it pays off.
- **Don't impose your style.** The goal is *their* work, better — more itself, not more like you.

## The two-sentence version

Find the point of view (or its absence), the generic parts, and the thing to cut — then show the better version of each, leading with whichever change raises the ceiling most. Stop when it's genuinely good, not when it's merely defensible.
