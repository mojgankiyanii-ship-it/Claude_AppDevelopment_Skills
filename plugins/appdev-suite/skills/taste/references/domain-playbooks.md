# Domain Playbooks

Taste is contextual. The faculties are constant (discernment, point of view, restraint, specificity, coherence) but the moves differ by medium. Read the relevant section for the medium at hand.

## Prose & copy

- **Voice before words.** Decide who's speaking and how before writing. Consistency of voice is most of the effect.
- **Concrete over abstract.** Nouns and verbs do the work; adjectives and adverbs are usually a sign you haven't found the right noun/verb. "Sprinted," not "moved quickly."
- **Rhythm.** Vary sentence length deliberately. A short one lands. Read aloud; cut what you'd never say.
- **Cut ruthlessly.** First drafts are 20–40% too long. Remove throat-clearing, restatement, and hedges.
- **Earn the claim.** Show the detail that justifies the adjective instead of using the adjective.
- **End strong.** The last line is the one remembered — make it the sharpest, not a summary.
- **Specific register.** Match diction to context; don't default to corporate-neutral.

## Visual & design

- **Point of view first.** Editorial / technical / warm / premium / playful — pick one and let it govern type, color, space, motion.
- **Hierarchy is the craft.** Size, weight, color, space create the 1→2→3 reading order before any decoration.
- **Restraint in color and effect.** One accent, used sparingly; soft consistent elevation; no decoration that lacks a job.
- **Type carries taste.** Scale, line length (60–75ch), weight contrast, tight tracking on display. One or two families.
- **Space is structure.** Whitespace and a consistent spacing rhythm read as quality more than any effect.
- **Reference deliberately.** Borrow a specific influence on purpose; don't assemble trends reflexively.
- (For implementation specifics, pair with a UI/UX craft skill if available.)

## Naming & branding

- **Evoke, don't describe.** A good name carries an image, a sound, or a slant meaning — not a feature list.
- **Say one true thing.** Taglines and positioning: a single specific claim beats "empowering X to do Y."
- **Coherence across touchpoints.** Name, voice, visual, and product should feel like one intelligence made them.
- **Distinctive but ownable.** Memorable and pronounceable and not a category description.

## Product & UX

- **Have an opinion about who it's for** and what it refuses to do. Taste in product is mostly *exclusion*.
- **Defaults are the product.** The out-of-box choices reveal taste more than the settings.
- **Subtract features.** A sharp small thing beats a bloated comprehensive one. Cut to the spine.
- **The whole flow, not the happy path.** Empty, loading, and error states are where taste shows.
- **Coherence.** Patterns repeat; the same thing has the same name and behavior everywhere.

## Code & API design

- **Taste here is clarity, not cleverness.** Readable beats clever; obvious beats surprising.
- **Names.** Precise, consistent, honest. A function does what its name says, no more.
- **Restraint.** Fewer concepts, fewer parameters, fewer abstractions. Delete code. The best PR is often net-negative lines.
- **Coherent surface.** An API reads as if one mind designed it: consistent verbs, shapes, error patterns. Principle of least surprise.
- **Right level of abstraction.** Not over-engineered for imagined futures, not under-structured for the real present.

## Recommendations & curation

- **Curate, don't enumerate.** A short, opinionated, well-reasoned shortlist beats a comprehensive list. Choosing *is* the value.
- **Justify with specifics.** Say why *this* one, for *this* person/context — not generic pros and cons.
- **Have a top pick.** Taste means committing to a recommendation, with the reason and the tradeoff.
